Fundamentals of GIS

Final Lab Report by Christina DeFabis

View Maps

Christina DeFabis

Christina is a Sustainability PSM student at Temple University. This course served as an introduction to ArcGIS Pro and GIS in general. Please view some sample maps below created in the 2026 Summer I semester.

Map #1

This map shows the density of homicides in police districts in Chicago. It was part of a larger problem that involved comparing the density of homicides with the density of Airbnb reviews in each district. The two maps were, unsurprisingly, quite opposite of one another. The spatial data showed that, for the most part, the districts with the lower homicide densities had higher densities of Airbnb rentals. Although the results indicated that, in general, tourists prefer safer areas, it also suggested that they are not totally deterred by crime, as some districts with moderate crime densities also had moderate densities of Airbnb reviews. The northwestern and southeastern parts of Chicago both had low homicide densities, but also had low Airbnb review densities, suggesting that safety alone does not attract more tourists. This solution was obtained by first using the Selection tool to create a layer of only homicides, and then using the Summarize tool to calculate the number of homicides. I then used the Calculate Field tool to calculate the density of homicides and the density of Airbnb reviews per police district, and finally used the Symbology tool to create two choropleth maps with the resulting spatial data, one being the map shown here of homicide density per police district.

Map #2

This map shows candidate areas for a new healthy food store in Philadelphia. The problem here was to find the best possible location for this healthy food store, based on the following criteria: it is a large contiguous area within a Philadelphia Empowerment Zone, it is within 2,000 feet of a Regional Rail station or other SEPTA route, and it is not within 1,200 feet of a farmers market or healthy corner store. Although the mapped solution shows 5 areas where the Empowerment Zones, SEPTA routes, and areas lacking healthy options intersect, Area #5 is the clear winner because it is the largest contiguous area, followed by Area #1. This solution was obtained by buffering all of the criteria layers, using the union tool on the two SEPTA and two food option layers, and intersecting the Empowerment Zones with the SEPTA layers to create an "inclusive" area. The Erase tool was used to eliminate the ineligible areas near other healthy food options. A Multipart to Singlepart operation was used to see all 5 records for the "inclusive" areas, and I also added an "area" field to the resulting attribute table, and calculated the geometry to get the area of each polygon.

Map #3

This map shows the parts of wards in Flint, Michigan that are most at risk for having poisonous tap water. The problem here was to pinpoint the specific areas where three different risk factors overlap. Those risk factors were defined as being within .3 miles of a TRI facility measuring water pollution over 10,000 tons, being within .3 miles of a river, and being within .2 miles of lead service lines. The mapped solution shows two water pollution "danger zones." These areas include a small polygon that includes Wards 5, 6, and 8, and a slightly larger polygon that includes Wards 5 and 7. This solution was obtained by first joining a TRI facility spatial layer with a data table recording water pollution. I then sorted and isolated the records for facilities with over 10,000 tons of water pollution, sorted and isolated the records for lead service lines, used the Buffer tool on each of the 3 "risk factor" layers, and finally intersected the TRI facilities, rivers, and lead service line layers, resulting in the two highest-risk areas shown on the map. I then did a Multipart to Singlepart operation on the resulting layer to view the records for both "danger zones," and lastly used the Union tool to combine that layer with the ward layer to view demographic information for each "danger zone."
